Why Under-Pacing Is a Diagnostic Problem, Not a Bid Problem

Everyone reaches for bid multipliers first. In most cases, the fix is upstream — in inventory, frequency caps or creative rotation. Here's the audit I run before I touch a single bid.

Akash Puri
Account Manager, Display · Dentsu
March 12, 2025 · 6 min read

When a DV360 line item is under-pacing, the instinct is universal: raise the bid. It feels like the right knob because it's the most visible one. But nine times out of ten, the pacing problem isn't a price problem — it's a plumbing problem, and paying more just buys you the same broken plumbing at a premium.

Here's the audit I actually run, in the order I run it, before I touch a single bid multiplier.

1. Read the frequency caps like a hawk

The first place I look is frequency capping. A cap of "2 per user per 24h" sounds reasonable on a slide deck. On the campaign floor, it means half your addressable audience is silently getting filtered out by 11 a.m. every day.

  • Widen the cap to 4 per 24h and watch delivery for 6 hours.
  • If pacing recovers, the cap was your ceiling — not the bid.
  • If it doesn't, you've ruled out one of the cheapest fixes and can move on.

2. Audit inventory availability

DV360 will happily under-deliver if the deal ID or inventory package you selected doesn't actually have enough impressions available at your bid. Check the Bids won / Bids sent ratio in the report. If you're winning above 40% of what you bid on, inventory isn't the constraint. If you're winning under 15%, you either need a wider inventory pool or a higher bid — but you now know which lever to pull.

Raising a bid without understanding the win rate is like turning the heat up on an empty pot.

The sneaky diagnostic: creative rotation

Even with capacity to spend, if your creative isn't rotating properly — say 80% of impressions are going to a single fatigued creative — click rates crater and DV360's optimiser downweights the line item automatically. Force even rotation for 24 hours, then check whether spend recovers before anything else.

3. Only now, look at bids

If frequency, inventory, and creative are all clean, then the bid is worth touching. Even then, I nudge in 8-12% steps, not 25% jumps — you want to find the elasticity curve, not just win at any price. Big jumps also skew the optimiser's own learning, which can hurt performance for days.

The philosophy under all of this: bidding is a symptom-level lever. Frequency, inventory, and creative are the causal ones. Fix the plumbing first, and you'll often find you never needed to open the bidding tab at all.

"Bidding is a symptom-level lever. Frequency, inventory, and creative are the causal ones."
